Leon Li

Student

I was born in 1998 in Chicago and soon after was named Leon Li. When I was about six months old, my parents brought me back to their homeland, Taiwan. For a better education, my mom and I left Taiwan and arrived in California in late July, 2016. My homeland, Taiwan, is an East Asian country located southwest to Japan and north to the Philippines. When compared with Japan, it’s not a big island, but it contains rich terrain and famous sightseeing attractions, such as Taroko Gorge and Taipei 101.

I used to at Cypress College High School Diploma Program, and I am longing for finishing my diploma as early as I can. For the reason that I want to be educated at the US, since Taiwan’s higher education is getting worse, and students who get their PHD can’t find any job, so my parents decide to send me out for education. Now I am an junior student in the University of California Riverside, majoring in Comparative Literature/ Chinese.

When I was a kid, I read The Journey to the West which is a picture reader about the comic adventures of a monk in Tang-dynasty China. It was not until I attended a lecture by Professor Anthony C. Yu, the novel’s English translator, that I realized the profundity of it. When I finished the eleventh grade, my parents sent me back to the US to get a better education. The summer before I started my own “journey to the West,” I read the novel in the original. I found that it was not only a historical and allegorical tale, but also a guide to life for me. In my first year back in the US, I encountered many challenges and often felt frustrated. In those difficult times I reread the novel and its philosophy, drawn from Chinese Buddhism, Daoism and Confucianism, inspired me deeply. It taught me that only by confronting adversity could I overcome it. I also took one of its chapter titles as my motto: “Mind ‘monkey’ returns to the Right; The ‘Six Robbers’ vanish from sight.” It strongly suggests that, if I set my mind firmly on one goal, all obstacles will disappear. The more I read the novel, the more I found that the traditional Chinese philosophy contained in it is overwhelming and fascinating. I hope to become knowledgeable enough to appreciate the work in all its complexity. Therefore, I’ve decided to major in Chinese and learn more about Chinese culture. I feel I’m well suited for my intended major. I have a good command of both English and Chinese, but more than that, I have a keen desire to delve deeply into Chinese literature, religion, and history, which I had no chance to explore before returning to the States. If admitted to your department, I believe it would be the first step toward realizing my long-term goal—to become a scholar of Chinese religious history.
